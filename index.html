Securing Healthcare Data

The Challenge

Imagine several hospitals wanting to collaborate on research but unable to share sensitive patient data due to privacy regulations. How can they analyze combined datasets without revealing individual records?

Healthcare data requires both security and utility. Blockchain technology provides immutable, transparent record-keeping, while homomorphic encryption enables computation on encrypted data without exposing sensitive information.

Author's Purpose: This educational resource by Bella Bao, Yale University master student, aims to bridge the knowledge gap surrounding blockchain and homomorphic encryption technologies, making these complex concepts accessible to healthcare professionals and technologists alike.

Blockchain Technology

What is Blockchain?

Blockchain is a distributed ledger technology that maintains a continuously growing list of records, called blocks, which are linked and secured using cryptography. Each block contains a timestamp and transaction data that is inherently resistant to modification.

Why Use Blockchain?

  • No Single Point of Failure: Distributed across many computers, preventing system-wide failures
  • Tamper Resistance: Changes require consensus, making unauthorized alterations nearly impossible
  • Auditability: Complete transaction history provides accountability and traceability
  • Disintermediation: Reduces reliance on central authorities and third-party validators

Key Limitations

Transparency vs. Privacy: While beneficial for auditability, blockchain's transparency means all transactions are visible to all participants—problematic for sensitive healthcare data.

Solution: Homomorphic encryption adds a privacy layer, enabling secure computation on encrypted data while maintaining blockchain's integrity benefits.

What is Homomorphic Encryption?

Homomorphic encryption is a form of encryption that allows computations to be performed on encrypted data without first decrypting it. The results of the computations, when decrypted, match the results of operations performed on the plaintext.

Key Features

  • Privacy Preservation: Data remains encrypted during processing
  • Secure Computation: Allows third parties to analyze data without seeing it
  • Data Utility: Preserves the ability to work with sensitive information
  • Regulatory Compliance: Helps meet stringent data protection requirements
